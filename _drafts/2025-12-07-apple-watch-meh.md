## My review of the Apple Watch Series 10.

I've worn various fitbits since 2017 or so. In August, I picked up an Apple Watch Series 10 for cheap at the end of the product cycle. I switched from Android to an iPhone earlier in the year, so it made sense to go full on Apple fan-boy. To some extent, comparing a smart watch to a step counter is apples to oranges. But, none the less...

My experience with the Apple Watch, so far, has been distinctly meh. There are things I like, but enough weak points that I don't love it.

### The Good

The screen is bright and readable even in direct sunlight, which is the biggest single improvement from the fitbit. Unfortunately, the lack of a bezel leaves the screen vulnerable to scratches.

The Apple Watch is comfortable enough to wear at night, something I never did with the fitbit. That's another big step up. The Sport Loop fabric-and-velcro band looks OK and is very light. If I was going to take the watch in the water, I'd probably get a silicon band as well.

The health related sensors on the Apple Watch are solid. I bought mine in Australia, so I bypassed the O2 sensor kerfuffle. But, is an O2 sensor all that useful? The watch tracks heart rate, ECG, and breathing.

Where health and other data is concerned, I have more trust in Apple's commitment to privacy. That's important. The idea of an ad company having access to my vitals 24/7 on top of email, search history, location... It's creepy. So, I give Apple credit for handling private data with cleaner motives.

### The Bad

As a step counter, the Apple Watch is supposed to be highly accurate. It has a weird quirk though. By default, it doesn't show step count on the home screen. You have to install an 3rd party app for that. And even then, the step count doesn't update constantly while you're walking. You have to press on the step count to get it to update. I guess maybe that's a battery saving strategy? The Fitbits, which could go several days between charges, updated as you stepped. It's weak that this supposedly more sophisticated device doesn't do that. And, is that free third party pedometer app collecting my data and selling it onward? Who knows? So much for better privacy.

Fucking up someone's sleep is rude. All smart watches I've ever owned would occasionally decide to light up in the middle of the night. The Apple watch does this too, which is annoying. Before I silenced "time to stand up" notifications, those would sometimes fire in the wee hours. No, I don't need to stand up at 4am. Also, when I'm sleeping, I don't give a shit if the battery is low. For a premium product to disturb your sleep for dumb stuff like that is pretty much unforgivable.

Speaking of notifications, who thought a constant stream of pinging buzzing notifications was a good idea even during waking hours? I already have the attention span of a hampster. Do I need more distraction? A _smart_ device should be able to learn which notifications I care about. I want notifications from my immediate family only. Work, only during working hours. Everything else of relevance, as a feed I can check when I feel like it. Know what I don't care about and send that crap straight to some dumping ground that I never have to look at.

The battery life is notoriously bad on the Apple Watch. The charging setup is nice, but I have to charge every day. Practically, that means the watch spends the night on the charger and I miss out on the sleep tracking. Otherwise, I have to think ahead charge while showering or during an extending period at my desk. I suppose short battery life is the price you pay for a good screen. I also wonder how quickly the battery's ability to hold a charge will degrade over time.

### Summary

The Apple Watch has a great screen, integrates nicely with the Apple ecosystem, and is maybe less exploitive of your data. But, for a mature higher-tier product, the rough spots are pretty glaring.
